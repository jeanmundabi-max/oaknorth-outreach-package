17B Deposit Economics — Understand It First (Plain English + Calculations) Cheat Sheet ›
Read this before Card 18. Card 18 has the scripted answers. This card explains what the words actually mean — so when you say them, you believe them.

What Does a Bank Actually Do?

A bank sits in the middle. It borrows money cheaply from savers — your business savings account. Then it lends that same money at a higher price to a property developer or a hospitality business. The gap between what it pays you and what it charges them is the bank's profit. That gap has a name.

Term 1 — NIM (Net Interest Margin)

Like a kid: Imagine you borrow £10 from a friend at 1% interest, then lend that £10 to someone else at 7% interest. You earn 6p for free. That 6% gap — that is your margin. Banks call it NIM.

OakNorth version: OakNorth pays a business saver 3.62% per year. It lends that money to a property developer at roughly 7–8%. The gap — approximately 4% — is OakNorth's NIM. On £7.2 billion of loans, every 0.1% of NIM improvement is worth roughly £7.2 million per year.

Remember this sentence: "NIM is the gap between what OakNorth pays savers and what it charges borrowers. Every deposit acquired through marketing widens that gap by reducing the bank's average funding cost."

Term 2 — Retail Deposits vs Wholesale Funding

Like a kid: OakNorth needs money to lend. It can get it from two places. It can ask businesses to park their savings with them — that is retail deposits, and it costs 3.62% per year. Or it can borrow from other banks and institutions on the wholesale market — that costs more, roughly 5–5.5% per year.

Every time a business moves £1M of savings to OakNorth, OakNorth does not need to borrow that £1M from the wholesale market. It saves roughly 1.4–2% per year on that £1M.

Calculation:
Wholesale rate: ~5.0%  |  Deposit rate paid to customer: 3.62%
Saving per year: 5.0% − 3.62% = 1.38% per year
On £1M deposit: £1,000,000 × 1.38% = £13,800 saved per year
On £5M total deposits acquired: £69,000 saved per year

Term 3 — Weighted Average Cost of Funds

Like a kid: If you borrow half your money at 3% and half at 6%, your average cost is 4.5%. Banks calculate the average cost of every pound they have borrowed — from savers, from other banks, from investors. That average is their "cost of funds."

If more pounds come from cheap retail deposits (3.62%) and fewer come from expensive wholesale markets (5.5%), the average goes down. Lower average cost of funds = more profit on the same loan book. Marketing that acquires deposits directly moves this number.

Term 4 — CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)

Like a kid: You spend £50,000 on a campaign. You get 10 new business savings customers. CAC = £50,000 ÷ 10 = £5,000 per customer.

For lending, you calculate CAC against the loan margin over the loan term. For deposits, you calculate CAC against how long the deposit stays and how much it saves the bank per year. That is why deposit CAC works differently.

The Three Calculations — Write These Down

Calculation 1 — The Acquisition Message (Rate Differential)

OakNorth rate:     3.62% AER
High-street bank: ~1.50% AER (HSBC easy access, confirmed)
Gap:             3.62% − 1.50% = 2.12% per year

On £1,000,000: £1,000,000 × 2.12% = £21,200/year → "over £21,000"

What the Hiring Manager Fears About Candidates

  • Someone who's done measurement but has never owned an acquisition number — will spend six months building dashboards and never launch a campaign.
  • Someone who doesn't understand FCA financial promotions — will have campaigns blocked every time.
  • Someone who thinks contractor work means taking time to figure things out — this role needs output in thirty days.
  • Someone who talks about "channels" but can't name a specific channel mix decision they've made and why.
  • Someone who treats marketing as a support function for BD — not a revenue driver in its own right.
  • Someone who can't connect deposit acquisition to NIM and the P&L — this is a finance-literate environment.

What Makes the Hiring Manager Say Yes Immediately

  • Candidate uses NIM language unprompted: "deposit acquisition is a P&L line, not a marketing KPI."
  • Candidate names OakNorth's specific competitive threats without being prompted — Revolut banking licence, Allica broker interception, FSCS consolidation window.
  • Candidate understands FD vs founder — who actually makes the cash management decision in the £1m–£100m segment.
  • Candidate names a specific data finding that changed a channel allocation decision.
  • Candidate demonstrates compliance is already in how they work — not something they learn on the job.
  • Candidate has a 90-day plan naming specific OakNorth products, specific target segments, specific metrics.
  • Candidate can answer: "If you were here on day one, what would you do first?" with a named campaign and a named target list.
The question the hiring manager asks themselves after the interview:
"Could this person have a campaign live by day 55 — and report it to the board in a way that makes sense?"

Your goal: Make them answer yes — because you've shown them exactly what campaign it would be, who the audience is, what the compliance process looks like, and what the CAC model will measure it.
